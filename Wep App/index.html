Curiosity

Chronometer or chronograph is the name given to a specific type of watch used to measure small intervals of time, usually down to milliseconds. The term, authorizations to designate any type of watch, a common reference to more precision devices. The first device was built in the 18th century as an equipment that can aid navigation. Its use would make it possible to determine safer and more accurate sea routes. Once accepted as marine instruments, improvements were introduced into the chronometers, so that the models gradually became very compact and accurate, similar to modern competition chronometers. Until the end of the 18th century, the instrument used to determine sea routes was the quadrant, which provided the location of a ship based on the relative position of the stars. For this, it is necessary that the process be covered at intervals of hours; another alternative was to use tables that indicated the location of the boat according to the time of day. Pendulum clocks of the time functioned satisfactorily only on dry land. At sea, the sudden changes in temperature combined with the swing caused by the waves affected much of the regularity of the pendulum movements, causing considerable errors in the measurement of time. Errors hindered the establishment of the route, which often led to serious accidents, such as that of 1714, in the Scilly Islands, near England, in which two thousand people died. To avoid further tragedies, a natural alternative was to develop a clock that could work on a ship's side and whose position could be calculated using special tables. John Harrison, a watchmaker in Yorkshire, known for the accuracy of the apparatus he built and the improvements made to traditional pendulum mechanisms, developing his first marine model in 1735. His subsequent models, the H2, H3 and H4, were decisive in development of the stopwatch we know, even winning a £20,000 prize from the British government. The H4, for example, brought many of the ideas that are still used today in the construction of watches. Much smaller and more accurate than its predecessors, it was the size of an ordinary alarm clock, and it had a spring system called a "remontoire", which made it possible to compensate for variations in the tension introduced by the string spring, another very reproduced device, this time in watches of cars. Despite the high probability of these chronometers, until the nineteenth century the use of these devices remained restricted to navigation, as their high construction cost did not justify their use for other purposes.
